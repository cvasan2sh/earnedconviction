FORGE PROBLEM STATEMENT — DATA ENGINEERING STARTUP OPPORTUNITY
===============================================================

PROBLEM:
The data engineering landscape is in transition. Mechanical work (movement, transformation,
orchestration) is being commoditized. A new layer of value is forming — but it is not yet
clear exactly where the highest-leverage, most buildable opportunities sit for a new entrant.

The question is NOT "what are the trends?" That is already known.

The question is: for a deep-tech startup entering this space TODAY, which specific problems
have all three of the following properties simultaneously:
  (A) ACUTE practitioner pain — people are suffering this NOW, not theoretically
  (B) TECHNICALLY FEASIBLE — buildable with current primitives, no research breakthroughs needed
  (C) MARKET GAP — incumbents are not solving it well, and the category is not yet crowded

The Forge must produce SPECIFIC candidate opportunities — not themes, not trend summaries.
Specific enough that a founding team could write a product spec from the output.


SCOPE CONSTRAINTS FOR THE AGENTS:
- Focus on 2025-2028 horizon
- Distinguish between "vendors are talking about this" vs "practitioners are actually suffering this"
- Distinguish between "someone is building this" vs "nobody has built the right version yet"
- Distinguish between "frontier teams have solved this" vs "median enterprise is still bleeding here"
- Do NOT summarize trends. The Crucible must produce candidates, not observations.


WHAT THE CRUCIBLE MUST PRODUCE:
- 3 to 5 specific startup opportunity candidates
- For each: name the exact pain, name why existing solutions fail, name what the right
  product looks like, name who the buyer is, and assess defensibility
- A ranked conviction order with explicit reasoning for the ranking
- The one opportunity the Crucible would bet on if forced to choose only one


AGENT INSTRUCTIONS:
===================

HISTORIAN:
Your job is to find the PATTERN, not recount the history.
- In each prior transition (ETL era → Modern Data Stack era → Lakehouse era), what class
  of problem got LEFT BEHIND and became the next startup opportunity?
- What is the structural analog today — what is the class of problem that the current
  transition is leaving exposed?
- What does the graveyard of failed attempts in this space tell you about what NOT to build?
- Specifically: which past startup categories got commoditized by the platforms, and which
  ones survived and why?

FIRST PRINCIPLES:
Your job is to reason from the CHANGED CONSTRAINT, not describe the landscape.
- The primary consumer of data has shifted from human analysts to AI agents and ML pipelines.
  What does that shift SPECIFICALLY demand from infrastructure that did not exist before?
- Data quality has always been a problem. Why has it NOT been solved despite $180B spent on
  data tooling? What structural reason keeps it unsolved — and does that structural reason
  change in the AI-consumer era?
- If you had to identify the ONE thing that makes AI agents fail on enterprise data today
  that is NOT a model capability problem, what is it — and is it buildable?

FALSIFICATION:
Your job is to KILL weak opportunities before the Crucible wastes time on them.
- Data observability (Monte Carlo, Bigeye) — is this category solved, crowded, or still open?
- Semantic layers (Cube, dbt Semantic Layer) — do these actually solve the AI-agent problem,
  or do they solve the human-BI problem? What is the gap if any?
- Data contracts — lots of talk, little enforcement. Is this a real product gap or a
  process/culture problem that tooling cannot fix?
- Cost optimization for cloud warehouses — is this a product opportunity or will Snowflake
  and Databricks just build it natively?
- For each: what would have to be true for this to be a real, durable startup opportunity
  vs. a feature that gets absorbed by the platform in 18 months?

CRUCIBLE:
You receive outputs from Historian, First Principles, and Falsification.
Your job is to SYNTHESIZE into specific, ranked, actionable opportunities.
Do NOT produce a balanced view. Produce a CONVICTION.
- What does the Historian's pattern predict that First Principles confirms?
- Which candidates survived Falsification — and which ones did Falsification kill?
- Rank the survivors by: (1) pain acuity, (2) buildability, (3) defensibility, (4) timing
- State the single best bet and WHY, with explicit acknowledgment of what could make it wrong.


PRACTITIONER VOICE — weight these above vendor/analyst content:
===============================================================

COMPLAINT 1 — ROLE AMBIGUITY / SCOPE CREEP (most frequent Reddit signal)
"Data Engineer is not well-defined and can mean wildly different things depending on the company."
Engineers routinely own security, infrastructure, microservices, analytics, AND pipelines.
Root cause: no clear boundary between data producer accountability and data consumer needs.

COMPLAINT 2 — BUZZWORD FATIGUE / VENDOR TRUST DEFICIT
Practitioners are hostile to: Data Fabric, Medallion Architecture, Zero ETL, Modern Data Stack.
Charge: rebranded old ideas with new pricing. Zero ETL moves complexity, does not eliminate it.
Root cause: vendors solve their distribution problem, not the practitioner's actual problem.

COMPLAINT 3 — THE SEMANTIC GAP (acute, no satisfying solution exists)
LLMs can infer data types but cannot understand intent:
  - "active users" = logged in last 30 days? opted into emails? paid in last 90 days?
  - "Amount" = net or gross? euros or dollars? including tax?
  - Which source is the golden record vs. the legacy artifact?
Business semantics live in people's heads. No tool has captured and operationalized them.
Root cause: semantics are organizational knowledge, not technical knowledge — hard to encode.

COMPLAINT 4 — SCHEMA CHANGE BLAST RADIUS (daily operational pain)
When upstream changes a column, downstream pipelines break silently or loudly.
Current workflow: pipeline fails → DE investigates → traces to schema change → fixes manually.
Entirely reactive. No proactive blast radius mapping. No automated impact analysis.
Root cause: data systems lack the equivalent of software's dependency graph + breaking change detection.

COMPLAINT 5 — DATA QUALITY AS ORGANIZATIONAL FAILURE
67% of professionals don't trust their org's data — UP while tooling investment INCREASES.
Tools are not solving it. Problem is organizational: unclear ownership, no producer accountability.
Root cause: data quality tools alert on symptoms; they don't enforce producer accountability.

COMPLAINT 6 — AI AGENT FAILURES UNTRACEABLE
When an AI agent produces a wrong answer on enterprise data, nobody can trace why.
Which training rows? Which embedding version? Which metric definition? Which source system?
Current lineage tools trace SQL. They do not trace embedding pipelines, RAG retrievals, or
model inference chains.
Root cause: lineage tooling was built for the SQL/warehouse world, not the AI pipeline world.


CONTEXT (background only — do not treat as conclusions):
=========================================================
- Traditional ETL vendors (Informatica, Talend) built empires on reliable data movement
- Snowflake/Databricks/dbt disrupted by moving transformation into the warehouse
- Apache Iceberg has emerged as dominant open table format — format wars largely settled
- Zero-ETL pushed by AWS/Snowflake as direct source-to-target; practitioners call it marketing
- Data contracts discussed extensively; implemented as runtime enforcement almost nowhere
- Primary data consumer shifting from human analysts to AI agents and ML pipelines
- Median enterprise: 60-80% of DE time consumed by pipeline maintenance, not new work
- "ETL is dead" narrative loudest from vendors who benefit from ETL's death
- Data observability market: Monte Carlo, Bigeye, Acceldata — crowded but not solved
- Semantic layer market: Cube, dbt Semantic Layer, Snowflake Semantic Views — BI-oriented
- MCP servers now available for all major warehouses — AI-data integration layer forming
- 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots failing to achieve ROI (MIT 2025 report)
- Practitioner trust in vendor narratives is LOW — weight complaints above analyst reports
